Hampi's roots can be traced back to the 14th century when it served as the capital of the Vijayanagara Empire. . This empire, under the leadership of King Harihara I and his successors, flourished for over two centuries.

Historical Significance: Hampi's history dates back to the 14th century when it was established as the capital of the Vijayanagara Empire by Harihara I. The empire reached its zenith under Krishnadevaraya in the 16th century and was known for its grandeur and cultural patronage.


Architectural Marvel: The ruins of Hampi boast a stunning blend of Dravidian and Indo-Islamic architectural styles. The city was adorned with magnificent temples, palaces, bazaars, and public buildings. The Vittala Temple, Virupaksha Temple, and Hazara Rama Temple are some of the iconic structures in the area.


Monolithic Marvels: Hampi is famous for its monolithic structures, including the massive stone chariot at the Vittala Temple complex and the monolithic statues of Narasimha (the man-lion avatar of Vishnu) and Ganesha.

Natural Beauty: In addition to its architectural wonders, Hampi is surrounded by stunning natural landscapes with boulder-strewn hills, lush banana plantations, and the Tungabhadra River flowing through, making it a picturesque destination.

UNESCO World Heritage Site: In 1986, Hampi was designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in recognition of its historical and cultural significance. The site attracts historians, archaeologists, architecture enthusiasts, and tourists from around the world.


Present-day Hampi: While the city itself is in ruins, the village of Hampi continues to be inhabited and serves as a gateway for tourists exploring the archaeological site. It offers various accommodations, restaurants, and services for visitors.


Hampi is not only a testament to India's glorious past but also a place of cultural, architectural, and historical importance that provides a glimpse into the grandeur of the Vijayanagara Empire.


The empire reached its zenith under the rule of Emperor Krishnadevaraya in the 16th century. During this period, Hampi became a center of art, culture, and trade, and it flourished with magnificent temples, palaces, and infrastructure. After the invasion, Hampi was largely abandoned and left in ruins. The once-thriving city fell into obscurity.

Today, Hampi stands as a remarkable archaeological and historical site, offering a glimpse into the grandeur of the Vijayanagara Empire and its architectural, cultural, and religious achievements.
